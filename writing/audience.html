Writers get stuck when they picture no reader, or an impossible one (a stern grader who already knows everything and is looking for mistakes). The fix is to picture a real reader and write to them. Once you know who you’re writing for, a hundred small decisions answer themselves — how much to explain, where to slow down, what to assume.

For your analytical writing: your class

When you write analysis in this course, your audience is your class — or all the 9th-grade sections. Picture that reader concretely. They are:

  • Familiar with the text. They’ve read it too. So you don’t summarize the plot or re-tell the poem — you can get straight to your argument and trust them to follow a quote.
  • Aware of the assignment. They know what we’re doing. So you don’t explain the task back to me (“In this essay I will…”); you just do it.
  • Smart, and skeptical in a friendly way. They’ll follow a real argument, but they won’t take your claim on faith — which is exactly why you need evidence and reasoning.

Writing to that specific reader is freeing: it cuts the throat-clearing (plot summary, assignment-restating, padding) and leaves room for the actual thinking.

For your narrative writing: write for the reader

Narrative memoir has a reader too, and the backbone of how we think about it comes from the Good Story Company’s piece “Writing for the Reader.” The core idea: a story isn’t just self-expression — it’s an act of communication, a thing you build so that a particular reader feels something. Read the post; it’s short and it’s the spine of this work.

Ask any draft
Until then, these general reader-questions work on anything you write — analytical or narrative:

Who, exactly, is reading this?

Not “an audience” — a specific person or group. Your class? A younger sibling? A stranger who wasn’t there? Name them.

What do they already know?

Everything they know, you can skip. Everything they don’t, you have to provide — or they’ll be lost. Most over-explaining and most confusion both come from guessing this wrong.

What do I want them to feel or understand by the end?

If you know the destination, you can tell which details earn their place and which are just you clearing your throat.
