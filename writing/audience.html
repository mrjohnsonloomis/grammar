Writers get stuck when they picture no reader, or an impossible one (a stern grader who already knows everything and is looking for mistakes). The fix is to picture a real reader and write to them. Once you know who you’re writing for, a hundred small decisions answer themselves — how much to explain, where to slow down, what to assume.

For your analytical writing: your class

When you write analysis in this course, your audience is your class — or all the 9th-grade sections. Picture that reader concretely. They are:

  • Familiar with the text. They’ve read it too. So you don’t summarize the plot or re-tell the poem — you can get straight to your argument and trust them to follow a quote.
  • Aware of the assignment. They know what we’re doing. So you don’t explain the task back to me (“In this essay I will…”); you just do it.
  • Smart, and skeptical in a friendly way. They’ll follow a real argument, but they won’t take your claim on faith — which is exactly why you need evidence and reasoning.

Writing to that specific reader is freeing: it cuts the throat-clearing (plot summary, assignment-restating, padding) and leaves room for the actual thinking.

For your narrative writing: write for the reader

Narrative memoir has a reader too, and the backbone of how we think about it comes from Rhiannon Richardson’s piece “Writing for the Reader” at the Good Story Company. The core idea: a story isn’t just self-expression — it’s an act of communication, a thing you build so that a particular reader feels something. Read the post; it’s short and it’s the spine of this work.

Richardson lays out four questions to ask of a memoir, to test whether it’s written for you or for a reader. These are her questions, in her words:
Who is it for?

“Here are a few questions that you can ask yourself about your memoir to guide you in assessing whether or not your story is currently written more for you or for readers at large.”

Why does this matter?

“The answer to this is usually linked to the theme of the reflection in your writing. If the answer is more specific to you, that’s probably a good indication of who you were writing for.”

Did you go deeper?

“Memoir takes on a more reflective tone than autobiography. Instead of recounting events from your life, you go an extra step and pull the deeper meaning of those events to the surface and analyze them on the page. You could write a story about getting called a racial slur at your cousin’s tenth birthday party, but the story about how getting called a racial slur at your cousin’s tenth birthday party was your first experience of racism and affected how you then approached the world and your interactions with other people is way more interesting and arguably marketable to a larger/different audience.”

Do people get it?

“Sometimes personal reflections can function like an inside joke. They’re specific enough to you that only you fully comprehend the meaning behind your experience. If you share the story with someone else and it doesn’t click, you have to ask yourself if it doesn’t click because you need to dig deeper or if it doesn’t click because the story is for you and not for the reader.”

Richardson, Rhiannon. “Writing for the Reader.” Good Story Company, goodstorycompany.com/blog/writing-for-the-reader. Quoted directly.
Ask any draft
And these general reader-questions work on anything you write — analytical or narrative:

Who, exactly, is reading this?

Not “an audience” — a specific person or group. Your class? A younger sibling? A stranger who wasn’t there? Name them.

What do they already know?

Everything they know, you can skip. Everything they don’t, you have to provide — or they’ll be lost. Most over-explaining and most confusion both come from guessing this wrong.

What do I want them to feel or understand by the end?

If you know the destination, you can tell which details earn their place and which are just you clearing your throat.
