When you prove a claim about a text, your evidence is the text’s own words — a quotation. The goal isn’t to drop a quote on the page and hope it speaks for itself. It’s to weave the quote into a sentence of your own, so it reads as one smooth thought — and then to explain it.

Dropped in vs. woven in
The single most common quotation mistake is the “hit-and-run” quote — a quotation left standing on its own, doing no work. Compare:
Three ways to bring a quote in
All three are correct. Pick whichever fits the sentence you’re writing.

Signal phrase, then a comma

Introduce the quote with a phrase naming who’s “speaking,” then a comma.
Frost writes, “Nature’s first green is gold.”Signal verbs: writes, argues, describes, calls, notes, observes.

Blend a fragment into your own sentence

Quote just the few words doing the work, folded into your grammar with no comma at all.
Frost calls the new leaves “gold,” something rare and precious.This is usually the smoothest — and the most analytical.

Full sentence, then a colon

When your own complete sentence sets the quote up, use a colon.
The poem ends on a flat verdict: “Nothing gold can stay.”

The punctuation that trips everyone

Commas and periods go inside the quotation marks

American usage, and the one people get wrong most.
“Her hardest hue to hold,” Frost writes.  → comma and period both inside the quotes.

Quote small

Quote the exact words that matter, not whole lines. A three-word quote you explain beats a full sentence you dump. Short quotes are easier to weave and easier to analyze.

Keep the words exact

What’s inside the quotation marks must match the text precisely — spelling, wording, and all. If you change or add anything to make it fit, that’s a job for brackets and ellipses (a skill for later); for now, choose a quote that already fits.

Then explain it — always

A woven-in quote still isn’t proof until you say why it supports your claim. That’s the reasoning — the sentence after the quote that connects it back to your argument. Evidence answers “what in the text?”; reasoning answers “so what?” Skip the reasoning and you’ve only quoted, not argued.

“Quote sandwiched, never quote stranded: introduce it, quote it, explain it. A quotation left alone on the page proves nothing.”
