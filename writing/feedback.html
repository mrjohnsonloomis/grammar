Iteration is the third principle of this course — polished writing is re-written, not written — and feedback is the engine of iteration. But feedback only helps if it’s usable. Buried under a hundred marks, nobody revises anything. So here’s how it works.

How my feedback works
Tap the card for the full philosophy.
“I want you to know I read your work — that I value the time, thought, effort, and vulnerability in it. Then: one or two big things to focus on, a few margin notes, a rubric. If something’s not clear, let’s have a conference.”
Running a peer review that helps

For longer pieces we’ll do brief, focused peer sessions. Done well, a classmate can see things you can’t see yourself — but only if the session is set up right. Here’s the shape:

Come with an excerpt, not everything

Choose the specific part you want eyes on — the paragraph you’re unsure of, the opening that might not land. Handing over a whole draft and saying “thoughts?” gets you nothing useful.

Ask for exactly the feedback you want

As the writer, you drive. “Can you tell what I’m arguing here?” “Does the ending feel earned?” A specific question gets a specific, useful answer. Your classmate’s reaction is one honest data point — not a verdict.

As the reader, report your experience

Be the reader you’d want. Root what you say in the assignment and what we’ve discussed — not vague taste. Say what’s working as specifically as what isn’t: “I got a little lost right here, because…” You’re a reader describing an experience, not a judge issuing a grade.

Be generous, but honest

Not telling a classmate something you noticed that could help them does them a disservice. You can be kind and honest at the same time — those were never in tension. Say the true thing, gently.

And when writing about it helps

After a draft or a peer session, a little metacognitive free writing — what changed in my thinking? what am I going to try next? — turns feedback into a plan. That’s the loop closing: notice, draft, respond, revise, and around again.
