Most people think writing is something you either can or can’t do, and that “real” writing is the neat, finished thing you hand in. This course rejects both ideas. Here, writing is a tool for thinking, a daily practice, and — for anything polished — an iterative process of noticing, drafting, and revising. You’ll do a lot of it, most of it low-stakes, and you’ll get better the way you get better at anything: by doing it and paying attention.

“When you write, there’s stuff going on in your brain that may be fundamentally different than if you were to talk it through. Not better or worse — but different, and valuable. We don’t tap into this enough.”
Three ways we think about writing

Writing is a form of thinking

Pen on paper makes connections and ideas that talking doesn’t. We do a lot of low-stakes, often-unseen writing so we can use that kind of thinking — and so we have a written record of what we thought, when we thought it.

Writing is a practice

Not a talent. A thing you do, keep doing, and improve at through reps. The notebook you carry every day, and the writing you do in it, is the practice.

Polished writing is iterative

Finished pieces are re-written, not written. Noticing → drafting → feedback → revision. The quality lives in the loop, not in any single sitting.

What we create

Three kinds of writing, all year:

  • Free writing in our notebooks — to think, to warm up, to debrief.
  • Writing that analyzes literature — building an argument from what we notice in a text.
  • Writing that reflects on experience — short narrative memoir, told as story.
