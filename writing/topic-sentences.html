Every analytical paragraph is built to defend one idea. The topic sentence is where you state that idea — a debatable claim about how the text works or what it means, put right at the top so your reader knows where you’re taking them. Everything after it exists to prove it.

The catch that surprises people: you don’t start here. A topic sentence is the last part of your thinking to arrive, not the first. It’s what a noticing — one honest observation from your inventory — becomes once you ask it a single question: so what does this add up to?

Fact or claim?
The most common broken topic sentence isn’t wrong — it’s just a fact. A topic sentence has to be something a reader could disagree with. Flip between these two.
From a noticing to a claim
The move that turns what you saw into something worth arguing:

Start from a noticing

Pull one real observation off your inventory — a pattern, an odd word choice, a repetition, a thing that changes. Not “what does the poem mean?” Just “what did I actually see?”

Ask “so what does this add up to?”

Look at your noticing (or a few of them together) and push past the observation to an idea. Everything in the poem is turning into something else → so the poem is about how nothing beautiful lasts.

Say it as one sentence

Compress that idea into a single sentence you could put at the top of a paragraph. Name the text and its author, and make the claim plainly.

Test it: could someone disagree?

If a smart classmate could read the text and reasonably push back, you have a claim. If nobody could disagree, you’ve written a fact — go back and ask “so what?” one more time.

Three traps
The weak topic sentences almost always fall into one of these. The fix is the same each time: make it arguable.

The plot summary

“In the poem, the leaves turn from gold to green and then fall.” This retells the text instead of arguing about it. Your reader has read it too — don’t summarize, claim.

The bare fact

“The poem is eight lines long and rhymes.” True, undebatable, and therefore not a topic sentence. Facts like this are useful — but as evidence, not as the claim itself.

The too-big

“This poem is about life.” So broad it can’t be proven in one paragraph — or disproven, which is the same problem. Narrow it until it points at this text doing this specific thing.

“A topic sentence is what your noticing becomes once you ask, ‘so what does all this add up to?’ You earn it — you don’t guess it in advance.”
