When you look at a painting, you have to stand at a certain distance to take it all in — then you step close to see the brushwork. Reading a text works the same way, and Overview & Inventory makes both moves on purpose. It’s the noticing practice underneath everything we do with literature. Crucially: you don’t have to figure out what a text means. You describe what’s there, and the meaning assembles itself from what you notice.

How it looks — a worked example

The classic version of this exercise (credited to A. Laber of Bard College) uses a photograph: Bill Brandt’s East End Girl Doing the Lambeth Walk, in which a girl in high heels shows off for friends in a bare schoolyard.

Overview (stage 1) — step back

“A girl shows off her high heels for friends in a schoolyard.” One sentence. General impression. No interpretation yet — just the feeling of the whole.

Inventory (stage 1) — get close

“She’s wearing mismatched clothes.” · “Her body faces the camera, but her eyes dart back at her friends.” · “The girl at the left is staring at the high-heeled shoes; she’s wearing the shabbiest clothes.” · “Are they fooling around in a schoolyard?” A messy list — observations and questions, no pressure.

Stage 2 — extend the noticing

Now you ask so what? “Her eyes dart back at her friends” becomes: though she’s posing for the photographer, she cares more what her friends think — the heels are about status. The observation grows into an inference. Do this across the inventory and an overview-2 emerges: the confident girl thinks she’s “going places,” but the grim setting reminds us many of these kids are trapped in it.

Your turn
Here’s a tiny public-domain poem to practice on. Write a one-sentence overview, then inventory whatever you notice — keep the list messy and honest. Then reveal a worked model and compare. (You can do this with anything you’re reading; a text is just easier to embed than a photo.)
“Trust that what you notice will have significance — almost always. You’ll have time to return to your first thoughts and keep thinking.”

Notice the kinship with the sentence strand: reading like a writer in grammar and inventorying a text here are the same instinct — look closely, name what you see — aimed at different targets. Noticing is the root of both strands.
