Analytical writing has a reputation for being a formula you fill in — thesis, three quotes, done. We build it the other way, evidence-first: it starts with reading, moves through noticing, and only then becomes an argument. The claim is the last thing to arrive, not the first — because your claim should come from the text, not get stapled onto it.

The order we actually work in

Read, then notice

Start with the text and an Overview & Inventory. Before any argument, you build up a pile of honest noticings — details, patterns, questions.

Ask “so what does this add up to?”

Look over your inventory. What do the noticings, together, suggest about how the text works or what it means? That answer — debatable, arguable — becomes your argument.

State it as a topic sentence

Compress the argument into one sentence at the top of the paragraph, so your reader knows the idea they’re about to see supported.

Prove it — evidence + reasoning

Bring back the specific noticings (now evidence) that support the claim, and after each one, write the reasoning that connects it to your argument. Evidence without reasoning is just quotation.

Watch one grow from a noticing
The class model, on Frost’s “Nothing Gold Can Stay.” It starts with a single kernel — one thing a reader noticed — and grows outward: the noticing becomes a claim, and the claim gets proven. Your part: drag each piece of reasoning to the quote it explains.
The kernel: one idea, two scales

You’ve met the kernel before, in the Sentences strand: a sentence’s kernel is its bare subject + verb — the seed everything else grows from. A paragraph has a kernel too, and it isn’t a thesis you decide in advance. It’s a noticing — one honest observation from your inventory. Ask it “so what?” and it becomes a claim; the paragraph grows from there. Same idea at two scales: build from the seed, don’t staple the parts together.

“You don’t decide what a text means and then hunt for proof. You notice — and the meaning assembles itself from what you noticed.”

Build your own
Now you. Draft an analytical paragraph about a text you’re reading — claim first, then evidence woven with reasoning. Then reveal the class model, plus a second, different reading of the same poem (proof that a good claim is debatable, not “the right answer”). Nothing you type is saved.
“A literary argument is a debatable claim about how a text works or what it means, backed up with evidence from the text and clear reasoning that explains the connection.”
