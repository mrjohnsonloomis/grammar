Memoir sounds enormous — a whole life on the page. Ours is the opposite: think small. We usually work in one or two paragraphs, so what you’re writing is a narrative moment, not a full story. A single scene, closely seen and honestly told, carries more than a summary of a whole year ever could. The skills are storytelling skills — and they’re the same ones, pointed inward, that we use to read other writers.

The three moves
Tap a card for each.
Telling vs. showing
The heart of narrative craft. “Telling” hands the reader your conclusion; “showing” gives them what you saw and trusts them to arrive at it — the way you did. Click through the pairs.
Revise a telling into a showing
The core exercise. Take a flat, telling paragraph and rewrite it so a reader feels the conclusion instead of being handed it. Cut the feeling-words; give us what a camera could catch. Draft yours, then compare with a model. Nothing is saved.
“Focus on storytelling, not describing. Something happens, and by the end, something is different.”
And always: who is this for?

A memoir moment isn’t written into a void — it’s written for a reader, and who that reader is changes what you put in, leave out, and linger on. That question is big enough to have its own page: Writing for the Reader (W5b) — it’s the backbone of this kind of writing.
