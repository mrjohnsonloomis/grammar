Grammar here isn’t a set of rules to avoid breaking — it’s a set of moves you can make. Every lesson follows the same arc: Notice real sentences → Name the moves an author made → Build your own → Apply it to your writing. Colors mark what each word’s job is, never just what it’s called.

Names — who? what? Acts — does? is? Describes — which? what kind? Situates — when? where? why? Connects — joined how?
The seven lessons
A sequence, but also a reference — jump to whichever move you need.
01 The Sentence Kernelsubject · verb · fragment Every sentence is someone or something doing or being something. Build two-word kernels, finish fragments, and grow a kernel without breaking it. → 02 What Verbs Demandobjects · complements · linking Some verbs need receivers; some link to descriptions. Objects, complements, and the demands each verb makes on your sentence. → 03 The Word Toolbox8 parts of speech · Austen · Douglass The eight parts of speech, taught through the slots you know — and five literary passages to highlight, click, and read like a writer. The site’s standing reference. → 04 Expanding the Kernelprepositional · appositive · expansion Phrases are bolt-on word groups that answer which, what kind, when, where, and why. The core exercise: take a kernel and grow it on purpose. → 05 Compressing with Verbalsparticiple · gerund · infinitive Participles, gerunds, and infinitives pack a second action into phrase-sized space. Core exercise: combine flat sentences into one compressed sentence. → 06 Joining Ideas: Clausescoordination · subordination · emphasis Coordination, subordination, relative clauses — joining whole ideas and choosing which one gets the headline. Same facts, different emphasis. → 07 Sentence Craftcapstone · rhythm · openers · revision Variety, openers, rhythm, end weight. Revise deliberately choppy passages using every move from the sequence — the on-ramp to paragraph-level writing. →
How the sentence moves stack
Top-down: start with a kernel, expand it, compress into it, join kernels together, then conduct the whole thing. The Word Toolbox sits underneath as the reference layer for every stage. Structure is drawn the way it is everywhere on this site: thin line = phrase, heavy line = independent clause, dashed = dependent. Click any node to jump to its lesson.
