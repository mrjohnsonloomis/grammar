Clauses & Sentence Types

A clause is a word group with its own subject and verb. Meet the two families — independent and dependent — then see how they combine into every sentence you'll ever write.

Phrase vs. Clause — The One Test
Before classifying anything, check for a subject + verb pair.

Phrase

A word group that acts as a unit but has NO subject-verb pair.

after the final bell No subject. No verb. Just a unit of meaning.

Clause

A word group that DOES have a subject-verb pair.

after the final bell rang Subject: bell. Verb: rang. Now it's a clause.
Phrase = no S+V  ·  Clause = has S+V

A Common Point of Confusion
The double life of when, where, and why

These words appear in the introductory lists for both adverb clauses (as subordinating conjunctions) and noun clauses (as interrogative words). The same word does a completely different job depending on context — and the only way to tell them apart is to ask what job the whole clause is doing.

Adverb Clause
"Call me when you arrive."
The clause answers when? — it modifies the verb call. When is a subordinating conjunction.
Noun Clause
"I know when the bus arrives."
The clause fills the DO slot — know what? → when the bus arrives. When is an interrogative word.
Adjective Clause
"I remember the day when we first met."
The clause describes day — which day? When is a relative adverb (like a relative pronoun).
The test: ask what job the whole clause is doing. Modifying a verb → adverb clause. Filling a noun slot (subject, DO, PN) → noun clause. Describing the noun right before it → adjective clause. The same logic applies to where and why.
