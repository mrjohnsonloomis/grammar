This course treats writing three ways at once — as a form of thinking, as a practice you do daily, and as an iterative process for making polished pieces. Two strands run side by side, and you work at both from day one. Neither sits above the other.

i.
Writing is a form of thinking
Pen on paper does something talking doesn't — it makes connections and ideas you can't reach otherwise. We write a lot, low-stakes, to use it.
ii.
Writing is a practice
Not a talent you have or don't. Something you do, keep doing, and get better at by doing. The notebook and the daily reps are the point.
iii.
Polished writing is iterative
Finished pieces aren't written; they're re-written. Noticing, drafting, feedback, revision — the loop is where the quality comes from.
One of two strands
Sentences
Our approach to grammar: sentences are things you build, not specimens you dissect. Seven lessons, each following the arc Notice real sentences → Name the moves → Build your own → Apply it. Colors mark what each word's job is.

How the sentence moves stack
Top-down: start with a kernel, expand it, compress into it, join kernels together, then conduct the whole thing. The Word Toolbox sits underneath as the reference layer for every stage. Structure is drawn the way it is everywhere on this site: thin line = phrase, heavy line = independent clause, dashed = dependent. Click any node to jump to its lesson.

Sources & About

This site teaches sentences as things students build, not specimens they dissect — sentence combining, expanding, imitating, and revising, with grammar named when a writing move requires it. That design follows the research consensus that construction practice improves writing where identification drills alone do not (Hillocks, 1986; Graham & Perin, Writing Next, 2007), and the sentence-composing tradition of Killgallon, Noden, Anderson, and Strong. Terminology follows the Holt Handbook (Third Course) so what you learn here matches what you'll meet elsewhere.
