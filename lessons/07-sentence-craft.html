Notice
Rhythm is a decision

Listen to London manage your breath: a long winding sentence about the trail and the timberland… then four words. “It was nine o'clock.” The short sentence lands like a stamp because the long one came first. Austen builds an entire philosophical windup so her sentence can drop onto two ordinary words: a wife. Douglass ends a paragraph on ignorant and lets it ring.

None of that is accident, and none of it needs new grammar. It's the moves you already have — expansion phrases, compression with verbals, ranked joins — deployed with three questions in mind: Where does each sentence start? How long is it next to its neighbors? What word does it end on?
