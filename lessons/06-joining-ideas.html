Phrase vs. clause — the one test

Before joining anything, be sure what you're joining. Every multi-word chunk in English is either a phrase or a clause, and one test decides: does it have its own subject–verb pair?

Phrase

A word group that works as a unit but has NO subject–verb pair.

after the final bell No subject. No verb. Just a unit of meaning.

Clause

A word group that DOES have a subject–verb pair — a mini-sentence inside a sentence.

after the final bell rang Subject: bell. Verb: rang. One word made it a clause.
Phrase = no S+V  ·  Clause = has S+V  ·  That single test decides everything.

A common point of confusion
The double life of when, where, and why

These words introduce both adverb clauses (as subordinating conjunctions) and noun clauses (as interrogative words) — and adjective clauses too (as relative adverbs). The same word does a completely different job depending on context, and the only way to tell is to ask what job the whole clause is doing.

Adverb clause
“Call me when you arrive.”
Answers when? — modifies the verb call. When is a subordinating conjunction.
Noun clause
“I know when the bus arrives.”
Fills the DO slot — know what? When is an interrogative word.
Adjective clause
“I remember the day when we first met.”
Describes day — which day? When is a relative adverb.
The test: ask what job the whole clause is doing. Modifying a verb → adverb clause. Filling a noun slot → noun clause. Describing the noun right before it → adjective clause. The same logic applies to where and why.
