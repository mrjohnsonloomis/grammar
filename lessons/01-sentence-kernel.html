Two words can be a whole sentence

Look at how much work two words can do: Ice melts. Birds migrate. She refused. Each one names a someone or something and gives it a doing or being — and the move feels finished. Jack London opens a famous story on a kernel nearly that bare: “Day had broken…” Emily Dickinson rides one for a whole line: “He kindly stopped for me.” Strip away every decoration from any sentence you admire and you will find this same two-part skeleton: a subject and a verb. Writers don't start from rules; they start from kernels.
